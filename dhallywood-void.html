Dhallywood's Urban Void: The Absence of the Middle Class on Screen

✍️ Faiyaz ul Karim 📅 April 2026 ⏱️ 5 min read
For decades, Bangladesh's big-screen heroes have typically come from villages or working-class Dhaka neighborhoods. As Dhallywood evolves, the city's burgeoning middle class is finally demanding to see its own values and complexities reflected on screen. [cite: 410, 466]

Mainstream Bangladeshi cinema has long prioritized "pastoral" tales and mass masala formulas[cite: 414]. Blockbusters from Beder Meye Josna to Monpura revolved around rural life or poverty dramas, largely failing to appeal to the urban middle class and driving city audiences toward Bollywood[cite: 411, 412].

Traditional Dhallywood themes often centered on rural landscapes[cite: 412].

City Dreams vs. Village Dramas

The gap is widening. Dhaka-born generations often skip local cinema altogether, as the familiar formula of machete-wielding heroes and item songs no longer resonates with white-collar viewers[cite: 416, 417]. Even attempts at urban settings, like Farooki's Doob (2017), faced criticism for excessive wealth portrayals that felt like "fantasy la-la land"[cite: 418, 437].

Utshob's Urban Awakening

Utshob (2025) changed the conversation[cite: 423]. Set entirely in Mohammadpur, it explores family tensions through local traditions and metro city soundscapes[cite: 424, 425]. Its success—trailing only action-heavy blockbusters—proves that urban, educated viewers are hungry for emotionally rich social dramas[cite: 428, 431].

Utshob (2025) proved there is a massive appetite for smart, city-centered stories[cite: 421].

The OTT Revolution

While theaters remain risk-averse, streaming platforms have become the champions of urban narratives[cite: 443, 462]. Since 2020, platforms like Chorki, Bongo, and Bioscope have boomed by targeting Dhaka's youth[cite: 444, 445]. Original content like Oshomoy and Last Defenders of Monogamy addresses metro commuting, corporate ethics, and modern romance—topics that rarely reach multiplexes[cite: 447, 448, 456].

As cultural critics urge, filmmakers must seize this moment, blending emotional realism with commercial flair, or risk losing another generation of urban moviegoers[cite: 468].
